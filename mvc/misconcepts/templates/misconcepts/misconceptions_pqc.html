Understanding the Difference Between DKQ and PCS

Clarifying Key Concepts and Common Misconceptions

QKD vs. PQC: Common Misconception

People often think "quantum cryptography" means it's secure against quantum attacks.
But QKD is not PQC. QKD uses quantum tech; PQC protects against quantum tech. Just because it says “quantum” doesn’t mean it’s secure against quantum computers. In short: QKD = encryption with quantum tech. PQC = encryption against quantum tech. Understanding this difference helps us prepare smarter for a quantum future.

QKD vs. PQC: Difference

Quantum Key Distribution (QKD) and Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC) are both related to the future of encryption, but they solve different problems—and are often confused.
QKD uses the laws of quantum physics to securely share encryption keys. It doesn't rely on math problems like today’s encryption does. Instead, it uses particles of light (photons) to transmit information. If someone tries to intercept the key, the quantum state changes and the eavesdropping is detected. But QKD needs special hardware (like fiber optics or satellites), making it hard to scale.
PQC, on the other hand, is software-based. It’s about developing encryption methods that can resist attacks from quantum computers—which could one day break current encryption like RSA. PQC works on regular devices and networks, making it practical and scalable for real-world use.

Diffie-hellman: Classical Cryptography Key Distribution

Alice wants to send a secret message to Bob. To keep it safe, she needs to lock it with a "key" — like a password. But Bob needs the same key to unlock (read) it. The big problem? How do Alice and Bob share this secret key without someone else seeing it? In classical cryptography, they need to find a secure way to share the key first. That could mean: Meeting in person and writing it down. Sending it by trusted courier. Calling on a secure phone. Once Bob has the key, Alice can lock her message, and Bob can unlock it. But if anyone (like Eve, the eavesdropper) gets the key, she can also read the message. So, the key distribution problem is: How do Alice and Bob share the key secretly in the first place? That’s why classical methods can be risky — if the key is intercepted during sharing, the whole system is broken.
