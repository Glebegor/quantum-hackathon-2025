QKD vs. PQC: Common Misconception

Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC) refers to cryptographic algorithms designed to be secure against attacks from quantum computers. PQC is implemented in software and can run on existing computers and networks, making it practical for widespread adoption. Its goal is to replace or supplement current encryption methods (like RSA and ECC) that could be broken by quantum computers in the future.

Quantum Key Distribution (QKD) is a method of securely sharing encryption keys using the principles of quantum physics. Unlike traditional key exchange methods that rely on mathematical problems, QKD uses quantum particles (like photons) to transmit information. If anyone tries to intercept the key, the act of measuring the quantum particles disturbs them, alerting the communicating parties to potential eavesdropping. QKD requires specialized hardware and is mainly used for highly secure communication channels.

People often think "quantum cryptography" means it's secure against quantum attacks.
But QKD is not PQC. QKD uses quantum tech; PQC protects against quantum tech. Just because it says ‚Äúquantum‚Äù doesn‚Äôt mean it‚Äôs secure against quantum computers. In short: QKD = encryption with quantum tech. PQC = encryption against quantum tech. Understanding this difference helps us to be better for a quantum future.

QKD vs. PQC: Difference

Quantum Key Distribution (QKD) and Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC) both address the challenge of securing information in the age of quantum computing‚Äîbut they approach it in fundamentally different ways, and are often mistakenly conflated.

QKD leverages the principles of quantum physics to securely distribute encryption keys. Rather than relying on complex mathematical problems (as traditional cryptography does), QKD uses quantum particles‚Äîtypically photons‚Äîto transmit key information. The key feature is that any attempt to intercept the transmission disturbs the quantum state, immediately revealing the presence of an eavesdropper. However, QKD requires specialized infrastructure such as quantum-enabled fiber optics or satellites, which limits its scalability and practical deployment.

PQC, in contrast, is a software-based solution designed to withstand attacks from powerful quantum computers. It involves creating new cryptographic algorithms that remain secure even if quantum computers can break current standards like RSA. Importantly, PQC algorithms can run on existing devices and networks, making them more accessible and scalable for widespread adoption.
